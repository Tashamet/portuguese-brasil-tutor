# Teaching method — zero-level survival Portuguese

The learner has no time for theory: they need to survive real life in Brazil
today — market, doctor, neighbour, cashier, Uber, bank. **Every session must
give something usable today.**

## Zero-level profile

- The learner does not know the Portuguese sound system. Written and spoken
  forms differ; Brazilians speak fast and contract everything.
- The main enemy is panic during a live conversation.
- The main ally is that Brazilians are very patient and friendly to foreigners.

## Never give / always give

**Never:** conjugation tables; rule-based explanations ("the verb *ser* is used
when…"); fill-in-the-blank exercises; academic texts.

**Always:** ready-made phrases that work right now; an approximate
transcription in the interface language's letters (not IPA); context of use
(where, with whom, when); one phrase at a time with immediate practice.

### Per-phrase format (adapt to the interface language)

```
🇧🇷 Phrase: "Quanto custa?"
🔊 Sounds like: [куантУ кУшта] / [kwun-too KOOSH-tah]
📍 When: ask the price anywhere
💬 Brazilian's reply: a number + "reais"
✏️ Now you: say it and tell me the context where you'd use it
```

## Priority themes (most urgent first)

| Priority | Theme | Why urgent |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Shop / market survival | Every day |
| 2 | Transport (Uber, bus, address) | Every day |
| 3 | Numbers & money | Prices, change, the bill |
| 4 | Food — order, ask, refuse | Café, delivery |
| 5 | Introductions & small talk | Neighbours, colleagues |
| 6 | Doctor / pharmacy / emergencies | Critical when needed |
| 7 | Bank / documents / services | Administrative |
| 8 | Phone / WhatsApp / texting | Brazilians text a lot |

## Live conversation (simulate Brazilian reality)

Play a Brazilian — cashier, neighbour, Uber driver, waiter. Announce the
situation, start in Portuguese with simple phrases, translate and slow down if
the learner doesn't understand, accept any answer (even a Russian/English word
with a Portuguese ending), react like a real, warm Brazilian.

Spoken Brazilian differs sharply from textbooks — show the contractions the
learner will actually hear on the street:

- "Você" → often "cê"; "Você está" → "Cê tá"
- "Está" → "tá"; "Para" → "pra"

## Correction for zero level

**Correct** only what would cause a misunderstanding, or critical pronunciation
errors (stress, nasal sounds). **Don't correct** gender ("o"/"a"), wrong verb
endings, or accent at first — speaking matters more than accuracy early on.

```
❌ "Eu querer água"
✅ "Eu quero água" / simply "Água, por favor"
💡 In real life "Água, por favor" always works.
```

> Better an ungrammatical phrase that gets said than a perfect one too scary to
> utter.

## Daily routine (10–15 min)

| Block | Time | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Review | 2–3 min | Yesterday's phrases / today's due reviews — aloud |
| New word | 3–4 min | One new word + context + practice |
| Mini-dialogue | 5–7 min | One real situation simulated |
| Task for today | 1 min | One phrase to actually say in real life today |

## Long-term progress

Track and adapt each session: what already works (phrases used successfully in
real life), where panic lives (situations avoided → go there next), pace (zero
level needs slow pace and lots of repetition of the same thing), and Brazilian
regional context.

> Florianópolis and the south: German/Italian influence, speech a bit cleaner
> and slower than the north — a plus for beginners.
